# Market Research Rules

Rules for Step 4 (Market Intelligence) to ensure every claim is sourced or transparently estimated.

## The Core Rule

Every factual claim about the market must have one of:
1. A cited source with URL
2. A calculation with shown methodology
3. An explicit "estimated" or "unverified" flag

There is no fourth option. Do not present generated analysis as fact.

## Market Sizing

### Acceptable
- "According to Grand View Research (2024), the North American dental sterilization market is valued at $X billion" + source URL
- "Bottom-up estimate: 25 DSOs x avg 400 locations x $4K/yr sterilization spend = $40M addressable" (methodology shown)
- "Market size data not publicly available for this specific niche. Proxy estimate based on [methodology]"

### Not acceptable
- "$80M+ addressable" (no source, no methodology)
- "The global sterilization packaging market is projected to reach $X by 2030" (sounds like a report but no citation)
- Any number that "sounds right" but traces back to model generation

### Methodology for bottom-up estimates
When industry reports aren't available, build the estimate from verifiable components:
1. Count the buyers (how many DSOs, hospitals, etc.) - cite source
2. Estimate spend per buyer - cite source or show reasoning
3. Multiply and show the math
4. Label clearly as "ClimateDoor estimate" not "market size"

## Buyer Segments

### What to include per segment
- **Named organizations:** Real companies/organizations, not categories. "Heartland Dental (900+ locations)" not "large DSOs"
- **Decision maker title:** Specific title from actual org charts or job postings, not generic
- **Procurement structure:** How do they actually buy? Centralized? Department level? GPO?
- **Sales cycle estimate:** Based on comparable sales data if available, labeled as estimate if not
- **ACV potential:** Show the math. "$4K/clinic x 400 clinics = $1.6M per DSO" not just "$1.6M"

### Sources for buyer data
1. Company websites (org structure, procurement pages)
2. LinkedIn (decision maker titles, team structures)
3. Government procurement portals (past contracts reveal buyers)
4. Industry association member directories
5. Conference attendee/sponsor lists (reveals who's spending in the space)

## Market Signals

### What makes a good signal
- **Specific:** "EU ECHA published PFAS restriction proposal on [date]" not "PFAS regulations are tightening"
- **Dated:** When did this happen or when will it happen?
- **Sourced:** URL to the actual regulation, announcement, or report
- **Actionable:** What does this mean for the company specifically?
- **Time-bound:** How long is this signal relevant?

### Signal categories
- **Regulatory:** New rules, proposals, deadlines, enforcement actions
- **Competitive:** Competitor funding, product launches, market exits
- **Procurement:** New RFPs, standing offer updates, policy changes
- **Market:** Buyer behavior shifts, adoption trends, technology changes

### Action levels
- **ACT NOW:** Time-sensitive, window closing. Must include a specific deadline or timeline.
- **KNOW:** Important context for strategy. Affects positioning or timing.
- **WATCH:** Developing situation. Monitor but no action needed yet.

## Competitive Landscape

### What to verify for each competitor
- Company actually exists (website URL)
- Product actually does what you're describing (from their own site)
- Funding data from Crunchbase or press releases (not guessed)
- Market position based on observable evidence (customer logos, case studies, partnerships)

### What NOT to do
- Don't invent competitors that "probably exist"
- Don't assume competitor pricing without evidence
- Don't describe competitor weaknesses without sourced evidence
- Don't claim a "first mover advantage" without verifying no one else is doing it

### Defensibility claims
Defensibility factors must be specific and verifiable:
- "Patent #X covers Y" (source: patent database)
- "Competitors would need 18-24 months to retool because [specific reason]" (reasoning, not just assertion)
- "70% trial-to-conversion rate" (from company data, flagged if self-reported vs. verified)

## Conference Targets

### Required fields (all from event website)
- Event name
- Exact dates
- Location
- Expected attendee count (from event site, not estimated)
- Registration status (open, closed, waitlist)
- Early bird deadline if applicable
- Why it's relevant (specific buyer/investor overlap)

### Do not include conferences that
- Have already happened (check dates against current date)
- Don't have a confirmed 2026 date yet (flag as "dates TBD")
- Are purely academic with no buyer/investor presence

## Unique Angle Research (ClimateDoor Differentiator)

Step 4 must actively research these specific angle types for EVERY company. These come directly from how the ClimateDoor team evaluates companies. If you skip these, the playbook will feel generic.

### Geographic Expansion Angles
For every company, check:
- **Singapore:** If the tech relates to buildings, energy efficiency, or urban infrastructure, Singapore's 2030 decarbonization mandate creates demand. Search: "[sector] Singapore 2030 decarbonization"
- **EU market:** Check if Horizon Europe has relevant calls. Check if LCBA program creates a channel. Search: "Horizon Europe [sector] call 2026"
- **First Nations / Indigenous communities:** If the tech is off-grid, modular, or relates to housing, energy, food security, water, or waste, there's likely an ICP4 angle. Search: "[tech type] indigenous community [province]"
- **Canada entry:** If the company is international, Canada market entry is a ClimateDoor core strength. Research Canadian regulatory pathways, ISED programs for foreign companies, provincial incentives.
- **Other geographies:** Based on the company's specific tech, are there countries with aggressive mandates that create demand? (Middle East solar, Nordic circular economy, APAC clean transport, etc.)

### National Security / Dual-Use Angles
For every company, check:
- **Website logo scan:** Look for DARPA, DND, Canadian Armed Forces, NATO, or defense contractor logos on their website. These indicate military traction (not just applications).
- **Why military traction matters:** "The military did due diligence and bet government dollars. That validates the tech for civilian use at a higher level than anything else." This is a green flag for investors, not a niche play.
- **ITB policy:** Canada's Industrial and Technological Benefits policy creates procurement pathways for companies with defense-relevant technology. Search: "Industrial Technological Benefits [sector]"
- **National security framing:** Critical minerals, food security, energy security, water security all connect to the national security agenda. If the company's tech touches any of these, note the angle.

### Grants-as-BD-Tool Pattern (MOST DIFFERENTIATED)
For every company, answer this question: "Can the company's END CUSTOMER get a grant that pays for the company's services?"

This flips the sales model. Instead of the company selling, their customer gets funded to buy.

Examples from the team:
- WildTac DNA (species detection): Indigenous communities and municipalities can get nature tech grants to pay for species detection services
- Clean energy installer: Homeowners and municipalities get energy retrofit grants that fund the installer's work
- Building technology: Property developers get green building grants that cover sustainability tech procurement

Research pattern:
1. Identify the company's end customers
2. Search grant databases for programs those customers can access
3. Check if the company's product/service qualifies as an eligible expense under those programs
4. If yes, quantify: "X grants available to Y customers totaling $Z potential"

### Competitor Sector Temperature
For every company, assess whether their sector is hot or cold RIGHT NOW:
- Search for recent funding rounds in the sector (last 6 months)
- Search for layoffs or shutdowns in the sector (last 6 months)
- Search for major policy changes affecting the sector
- Synthesize: "Sector is [hot/warming/stable/cooling/cold] because [specific evidence]"

Specific data points to find:
- Largest competitor's most recent round: who invested, how much, when
- Any competitor layoffs, pivots, or shutdowns
- VC activity in the space: is money flowing in or pulling back?
- Government policy direction: supportive, neutral, or hostile?

### Personal Rapport Building
For the specific person the ClimateDoor team is meeting:
- LinkedIn profile: current role, career trajectory, shared connections
- Personal interests visible on social media (sports, hobbies, volunteer work)
- Shared conference attendance with ClimateDoor team members
- Mutual connections in the ClimateDoor network
- Geographic connections (same city, same school, etc.)

Output as a brief "Rapport Notes" section, not integrated into the main intelligence. This is for the person running the call, not for the playbook itself.
