# Company Evaluation Framework

Source: ClimateDoor team evaluation session, Feb 26, 2026. This captures how the team ACTUALLY evaluates a company before Call 1. The synthesis step (Step 6) must think like this, not like a generic strategy consultant.

## Input Data Required

Before the research agent runs, the intake form provides:
- Company name and domain URL
- Contact name, job title, role, email
- ClimateDoor lead owner
- Lead source (referral, event, inbound) + referral name or event name
- City, state/province, country
- ICP1 type (growth stage vs. early stage)
- Primary climate sector (from ~10 options)
- ICP1 climate sector type (deeper multi-select classification)
- Sector served (who is the BUYER of their technology)
- What they're looking for (capital, marketing, grants, BD, partnerships)
- Elevator pitch (1-2 sentences)
- Venture stage, TRL level, revenue
- Why it's interesting to us
- Advantages we have
- Additional notes

## The Evaluation Hierarchy (in priority order)

### Priority 1: Match Against ClimateDoor's Own Network

Before ANY web research, query internal databases to find:

**ICP2 Investors:** If the company needs capital, pull all investors from HubSpot tagged by ICP2 that match sector, stage, geography, and check size. The power is showing REAL NAMES of investors we already know, not generic fund descriptions.

**ICP3 Buyers:** Pull all buyer contacts and organizations from HubSpot that purchase the type of technology this company makes. Show actual companies and actual procurement contacts. "We already know the VP of Procurement at Heartland Dental" is worth more than "DSOs are a target segment."

**ICP5 Experts/Ambassadors:** Pull domain experts from the expert database who are:
- Domain experts in THIS sector (e.g., circular economy, medtech)
- Domain experts in the CUSTOMER the company sells to (e.g., healthcare procurement)
- Domain experts in the GEOGRAPHY the company wants to enter

**Connection paths:** Similar to BD Hunter, identify how we can get introduced. Conference overlap, mutual connections, co-investor relationships, geographic proximity.

The goal: "Show up to Call 1 more prepared than any other business trying to sell them. Come with actual tangible, real names, real examples, real ideas, real potential sales targets or channels."

### Priority 2: Unique Angles (the ClimateDoor Secret Sauce)

This is what separates ClimateDoor from every other advisory firm. The team explicitly identified these patterns:

**Geographic opportunity angles:**
- "You're a building technology. Have you thought of Singapore? They're trying to decarbonize all buildings by 2030."
- "You're an off-grid modular solution. Have you thought about First Nations communities?"
- "Have you explored Europe? Canada-EU Horizon Europe is the largest cleantech grant program in the world."
- International companies looking to enter Canada is a core strength.

**National security / dual-use angles:**
- Look for military/defense traction on their website (DARPA logos, DND partnerships)
- Military traction =/= military applications. Military traction means "the military did due diligence and bet government dollars, which validates the tech for civilian use cases too."
- Critical minerals, food security, energy security all connect to national security agenda
- "Have you ever thought about food security as national security?"
- ITB (Industrial and Technological Benefits) policy creates procurement pathways

**Grants as BD tool for their CUSTOMERS:**
- Not just grants for the company. Grants that the company's END CUSTOMERS can get to then pay for the company's services.
- Example: WildTac DNA makes species detection tools. Their customer (indigenous communities, municipalities) can get nature tech grants to pay for WildTac's services.
- This is a unique angle that most advisory firms never think of.

**Competitive sector temperature:**
- Scan whether their sector is hot or cold RIGHT NOW
- "Hydrogen fuel cells? Ballard is laying off. Sector is cold."
- "Critical minerals? Hot as shit right now."
- Show competitor funding rounds: "Your biggest competitor just raised $20M from [fund]. Here's what that means for you."

**White space analysis:**
- Once competitors are identified, find what features the company has that competitors DON'T
- Map those unique features to specific buyer segments in specific geographies
- "Your unique factor is X, so there might be interest from Y buyers in Z geographies"

### Priority 3: Traction Signals

**Website logo scanning:**
- Go to the company's website and look for logos of companies they've worked with
- Government logos = government BUYERS (not just government funding). This is a huge green flag.
- Enterprise logos = validated at enterprise level
- Accelerator/incubator logos (Y Combinator, Techstars, etc.) = validated by selection process

**News signals:**
- Recent press releases or news about the company
- Regulatory changes in their sector
- Industry news that creates urgency or opportunity
- Competitor news (funding, partnerships, layoffs)

**Competitor intelligence:**
- Who are the company's top 2-3 competitors?
- What traction have those competitors had recently?
- Who funded the competitors and how much?
- Where is the competitive white space?

### Priority 4: Personal Rapport Building

**Contact research:**
- LinkedIn scan for personal interests, hobbies
- Mutual connections
- Shared conference history
- Shared geographic connections

## Critical Principle

"The goal is to build trust in Call 1 by showing we have all this already. We're showing up more prepared than any other business trying to sell them. This exists because of our intelligence layer and our ability to do this BEFORE Call 1."

Every piece of intelligence in the playbook must serve this purpose. If it doesn't make the client say "how did they know that?", cut it.
